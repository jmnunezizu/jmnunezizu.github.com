Record Shopping In Japan Take #1

2 September, 2017
Record Shops
Comments

I visited Japan for the first time in October 2014. I knew that Japan was big for record shops –and any collectable for the matter– but what I found really surprised me.

I flew from London with my wife on a direct flight to Tokyo Haneda. My flight landed at about 7:20am after a 12-hours flight. By the time we landed it was nearly midnight (London time). My hotel was booked in Shinjuku; a lively area that is well connected and has a bunch of Disk Union branches nearby. Getting there from the airport was quite simple really; a qucik ride on the Tokyo Monorail to Hamamatsucho and then a snappy change on the JR Yamanote Line.

We made it to the hotel and left our luggage there until check-in time. At that point, I started to feel my circadian rhythm all over the place. It was time to go for a walkabout: explore the area and get the much-needed coffee!

Shinjuku is one of the busiest (if not the) areas in Tokyo, but it’s surprisingly calm during early hours—especially on a Saturday morning!

After a short walk, we managed to find a Starbucks which was located on the second or third floor of some sort of gallery. The gallery seemed to be closed, but you could walk in. We found the lift and proceeded to head to the Starbucks floor. This is where everything got really interesting. No matter what we did on the button panel, the lift would completely ignore us and shout something in japanese that we obviously couldn’t understand. The doors would open and there it was the lobby. Doors closing, shout something again, ding and there it was: the lobby again. It was about 2am for us, so the laughter started to kick-in—which made the whole situation even better! At one point someone joined us, did something on the button panel that made our new enemy happy and off we went! I don’t know how much time we spent on that lift, but it definitely felt like ages!

Having finished the most-incredible cup of coffee, we decided to go for another walk. “What can we do?”“ I asked. “perhaps we could go to Disk Union, it’s only a 2-min walk from here” I said.

Disk Union was established in 1967, and is a network of record shops spanning Shinjuku, Shibuya, Ochanomizu, etc. Some shops could be seen as genre-specific boutique-style, while others are general shops catering to larger audiences.

The Disk Union Punk Store was the closest one, but unforunately was still closed. The Heavy Metal shop was nearby, and it was about to open. This shop is located on the 5th floor of the Mitsumine building. When we got there, we saw some people queuing outside the entrance. Some guy from the shop would peep on us from the window (which was covered with a curtain), would then come out and let some in. We din’t understand what was going on, but some quick-finger-browsing action could be heard. A few minutes later, the same guy would peep again, come out, pick someone else, and let them in. At that point we didn’t really understand why on earth we couldn’t get in. Pick me! Pick me!—my face would’ve probably screamed. “I made it all the way to Japan and I’m dying to get into a record shop!” The guy came out again, and this time, we made it through. Honestly, I felt distinguished, like having winning a prize—such an honour. And then I saw it. My word, the speed with whom japanese record-buyers flick through the records is something I’ve never seen before. And that lifting-then-dropping browsing style too. Wow.

I quickly positioned myself in front of one of the racks and started browsing. I’m not a heavy metal person. There’s only the odd record that I like, but Kiss is something that takes me back to when I was 12 years old. I did not want to buy much; I had only been in Japan for a few hours for crying out loud! And then I succumbed. Destroyer was going to be my first purchase in Japan.

When I left the shop I understood why all the fuss with letting us in: there was a sale happening that day!

Next up: Disk Union Shinjuku Flagship store—8 floors! I had opened the door and didn’t have any plans on closing it!

All Disk Union shops are located very close to each other. I started going floor by floor and my eyes could not belive what I was seeing. There were a lot of records that I have never seen before in my life. If that wasn’t enough, the other impressive feature is that you can actually hold the items in your hand, so I do remember spending a lot of time gazing at those records and just saying wow. One thing is to go to a record shop and see those rare or hard to find records on the wall or behind the counter; but another completely different thing is to find a lot of these in the racks. So much history at the end of your fingertips. It’s definitely something that you don’t experience every day!

I do remember feeling an urge for buying absolutely everything, but on the other hand the phrase “hey, you just got here, take it slowly” kept ringing in my head. What was extremely surprising, was finding a lot of UK pressings that were cheaper than buying them in the UK. I wasn’t expecting that. Surely there was going to be an import or shipping cost attached to it, right? Perhaps I was just lucky.

I guess that the hightlight for that day was buying in-shop—not online—Siembra by Willie Colon & Ruben Blades. I have another copy which belonged to my father but it’s in a really poor condition. This copy is in excellent shape. It’s not the Gatefold cover, I know—that one is still in my wantlist. If you haven’t listened to this LP, give it a listen. It sounds incredible and the musicianship is out of this planet.

We went back to the hotel for a well-deserved nap just around check-in time, with some Disk Union bags carrying 7 LPs. The highlights of that initial haul included English Settlement by XTC, Do It Yourself by The Seahorses, and the original UK pressing of The Hour Of Bewilderbeast by Badly Drawn Boy—yes, the one with the Andy Warhol photograph in the cover.

We woke up at about 7pm. We went for some tempura and then for another walk, which ended in…yes, you guessed it: Disk Union! Tokyo never sleeps, and record shops usually close at 9pm (some even stay open until midnight). I guess that staying in the Shinjuku area has its benefits!

The last visit to Disk Union ended up with a haul of 4 LPs, which included the hightlight Metiendo Mano! by, again, Willie Colon & Ruben Blades. This is the LP that includes the incredible Pablo Pueblo tune.

I was going to stay in Japan for another 15 days. Osaka was still to be visited. This was just the beginning.
